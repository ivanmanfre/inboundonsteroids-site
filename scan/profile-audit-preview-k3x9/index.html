The Fjällräven Trail Challenge visual identity started from a single physical object.

A collectible embroidered patch. Circular, fox curled inside, the hero line running along the bottom arc. Once we landed that object, a lot of the rest followed from it, the chromatic signature, the tactile feel, the overall register of the campaign. Crafted, badge-driven.

From there we built outward into operating rules: color governed by proportions, typography with assigned roles, two graphic motifs that travel together across every surface, an imagery direction that has to hold whether you're inside a store, out on a trail, or surrounded by people.

34 stores across North America, 10 curated hikes per location, 340 trails in total, all running through the same visual logic.

Fjällräven has been in outdoor design since 1960, present in over 40 countries. Getting the campaign system to feel like its own thing and still unmistakably belong to that history required an anchor, something physical and specific enough to define the rest.

The gift box character that waves at you in a welcome email is the same one celebrating when your package arrives.

We built Hey Dear's visual identity around a character set designed to carry different emotional states across the customer journey. The range runs from friendly in the welcome flow to nervous in the abandoned cart sequence to full celebration after purchase. When the character shifts with the moment, the brand's presence across the relationship feels earned and consistent at the same time.

Bold display type mixed with hand-drawn lettering, warm photography in pinks, purples, and oranges, small animations that give the illustrations a pulse. Every layer around the characters was built to reinforce the same feeling, so the system held across every touchpoint.

And then it crossed into physical: the stickers use the same characters, the branded tape seals the box. You open the email and you open the box and the brand is the same in both.

Moving one element 4px can change the entire composition. There's no rational explanation for why the version at one position looks wrong and the version 4 pixels to the right looks right, but it does.

This is the part of visual design that's hardest to explain to anyone outside it. You're making micro-decisions about spatial relationships, optical balance, visual weight, and the only instrument you have is your own perception of whether it's working. You adjust, look away, come back, adjust again.

The compositions that end up feeling correct usually carry invisible corrections like this, none of which show up in a revision log. I've never figured out a system for knowing when something is off before I try moving it. Most of the time I just try the move, see whether it clicks, and either accept it or push the element somewhere else.
